The Puppet & The Self 2025

How might we reduce self-esteem decline in children?

I researched to understand if and when there is a dip in self esteem in children, found a dip just before the onset of the teen years. Wanting to reduce this drop, I designed a participatory intervention that used school students in both puppeteering and audience roles. The intervention successfully introduced better self-concepts to different extents in both.

The Problem
Through primary research, I noticed a decline in children's self-perception right before they hit their teenage years. Interventions were needed, but they had to be more engaging than clinical.
The Solution
I designed a participatory puppetry intervention to build emotional resilience. I recruited school students to serve as both the audience and the puppeteers.
The Impact
The audience found a relatable, safe medium to process their insecurities, and the puppeteering helped the student performers overcome stage fright and build tangible self-confidence.

The Problem

Why This Project?

  • Self-esteem influences long-term wellbeing, resilience, relationships, and performance.
  • Existing interventions are often clinical, resource intensive, and difficult to scale in schools.
  • There was limited work exploring design-led interventions for self-esteem development in Indian children.

Design Challenge

How might we help children develop healthier self-perceptions through an engaging, accessible, and scalable intervention?

Understanding Self-Esteem

There are many ways to think about self-esteem and self-concept. Early theories often treated self-esteem as a single measure of self-worth, but more recent research recognises it as multidimensional. This means children can feel confident in some areas of life while struggling in others.

For this project, Susan Harter's multidimensional framework was used. Harter breaks self-esteem down into six domains: Scholastic Competence, Social Competence, Athletic Competence, Physical Appearance, Behavioural Conduct, and Global Self-Worth. It is important to note that these domains reflect the subject's perception of themselves within each area rather than their actual competence or performance. This framework was selected because it captures children's lived experiences, and helps identify where self-esteem challenges exist, rather than simply whether they exist.

Understanding Local Self-Esteem Trends

To understand where self-esteem challenges existed in children at a nearby school, I adapted Susan Harter's SPPC (multidimensional measurement tool) for an Indian audience (MSPPC). I then administered the survey across XXX participants from ages X–Y.

  • Insight 1: Self-perception declines significantly after age 12.
  • Insight 2: Children of different genders displayed different trends in each of the 6 domains.
  • Insight 3: The domains with the largest declines are: Social Competence, Physical Appearance, Scholastic Competence, and Athletic Competence.
Ideating a Solution

There was a need to design an intervention to help children reflect on their challenges and relate to others' experiences to build healthier self-perceptions before the decline at 12. Multiple formats including workshops, games, and storytelling activities were explored. Puppetry was ultimately selected because it combines storytelling with emotional distance. This would help children engage with sensitive topics through characters rather than by themselves.

Designing and Building the Solution

Character design was grounded in Inclusive Design principles. Each character was crafted to be relatable yet diverse in personality, colour, religion, and gender, so a wider range of children could see themselves in the narrative. Their hobbies and interests were also chosen to align with their personas. Each character had one "downfall." These downfalls corresponded to lower scores on different subscales from the Self Perception Profile for Children (Harter, 2012). To keep the show simple, only four downfalls and four characters were created instead of six. The subscales they corresponded to were Social Competence, Physical Appearance, Scholastic Competence, and Athletic Competence.

The script was designed to use four interconnected storylines (Diagram 1). This design helps the audience flow between different domains of self-concept. Each story focuses on one character's insecurities. Each story follows a clear structure (Diagram 1): setup (identifying the character's challenge), conflict (a situation that tests the character emotionally), and resolution (learning or transformation). Together, these stories aim to model emotional resilience and show the audience how to push through their own challenges.

The puppets were made with crochet for an approachable feel. The designer intentionally modified the base patterns to contrast conventional beauty standards and represent diverse body types. Cotton yarn was chosen for its durability and ease of use. Three different colours of yarn were used for skin types, and two thicknesses of black yarn were used for the hair. The thicker yarn was used to exaggerate the size of a character's (Tanvi's) hair to represent her insecurity.

The outfits were fabricated from scrap cloth using a sewing machine, hand stitching, and fabric glue. The clothes were designed to fit the puppets well and reflect each character's personality and psychological state. For example, socially anxious Aachal wore darker, more inconspicuous clothing, while creatively inclined Aariz wore brightly coloured block-printed clothing. The clothes were also made to be durable so they would not tear during many rehearsals and practice sessions.

The miniature puppet theatre is built for live performances out of light plywood. A CNC machine cut the pieces to high accuracy, and they were assembled by hand. The theatre is a compact rectangular box in the shape of a traditional stage, sitting between two raised platforms, like desks. The puppeteers sit behind and slightly below the set, hidden from the audience by a cloth. Long handles are inserted from below, with the sides used to move the puppets across the stage. The design prioritised minimising visibility of human puppeteers to maintain the illusion of the narrative world. The theatre had the following components (Diagram 2).

  • Rear Panel (a): This is a solid backboard with one thin horizontal slit. Puppeteers look through this slit during practice to check puppet positions. During the final show, background art covers the slit so the audience does not see it.
  • Side Panels (b): The left and right panels have wide vertical slots that line up with thinner slots in the base. Puppets enter the stage through these side openings with the handles extending past the base. This keeps human hands out of the audience's view. These panels also have small holes near the top corners for a curtain rod.
  • Base Panel (c): The base is made of three long boards with gaps between them. The puppet handles slide through these gaps. The base also features grooves that align with the top panel to hold scenery elements in place. It is built with two glued layers for extra strength.
  • Top Panel (d): This panel has multiple thin, parallel slots. Scenery elements drop into these slots from the top, making it easy to change scenes.
  • Connecting Supports (e): Small, cubical wooden braces are placed at the inside edges. They hold the corners together and keep the theatre sturdy.
  • Puppet Handles (f): Each puppet is securely sewn onto a long wooden handle. This handle reaches down below the set where the puppeteers sit.
  • Scenery Elements (g): These pieces are made of paper prints glued to MDF boards and are used as backgrounds for context and environment setting. They drop down through the thin slots in the top panel. The grooves in the base panel hold them securely in place. This design makes it quick and easy to change the backgrounds during the show.

Design Process

Puppetry emerged as the intervention medium through co-design sessions with teachers and students. The participatory format — where children both created and performed the puppets — addressed two audiences simultaneously: the puppeteers built agency and rehearsed vulnerability in a safe container, while the audience found characters and stories that reflected their own experiences.

Scripts were co-written with students over four workshop sessions, iterating on narrative arcs that modelled healthy emotional responses without feeling prescriptive. Puppet design was intentionally abstract to allow projection and identification across a range of self-concepts.

Outcomes

Post-intervention surveys using the Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale showed a measurable improvement in self-concept scores in both the performer and audience groups. Qualitative feedback highlighted themes of recognition, normalisation of insecurity, and a new vocabulary for discussing feelings.

The project was recognised at XXXX Conference and the methodology has since been adapted for use by the school's counselling programme.
