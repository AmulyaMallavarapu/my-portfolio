The Puppet & The Self 2025

How might we reduce self-esteem decline in children?

I researched to understand if and when there is a dip in self esteem in children, found a dip just before the onset of the teen years. Wanting to reduce this drop, I designed a participatory intervention that used school students in both puppeteering and audience roles. The intervention successfully introduced better self-concepts to different extents in both.

The Problem
Through primary research, I noticed a decline in children's self-perception right before they hit their teenage years. Interventions were needed, but they had to be more engaging than clinical.
The Solution
I designed a participatory puppetry intervention to build emotional resilience. I recruited school students to serve as both the audience and the puppeteers.
The Impact
The audience found a relatable, safe medium to process their insecurities, and the puppeteering helped the student performers overcome stage fright and build tangible self-confidence.

The Problem

Why This Project?

  • Self-esteem influences long-term wellbeing, resilience, relationships, and performance.
  • Existing interventions are often:
    • Clinical
    • Resource intensive
    • Difficult to scale in schools
  • There was limited work exploring design-led interventions for self-esteem development in Indian children.

Design Challenge

How might we help children develop healthier self-perceptions through an engaging, accessible, and scalable intervention?

Research

The research phase began with a literature review on self-esteem development in children aged 8–14, identifying a consistent dip around ages 10–12 across multiple studies. This was followed by in-school interviews and observational sessions to ground the academic findings in local context.

Primary research involved structured activities with 60+ students across two Hyderabad schools. Story completion tasks, drawing exercises, and group discussions surfaced the specific nature of self-esteem struggles: social comparison, academic pressure, and appearance concerns were most prominent.

Design Process

Puppetry emerged as the intervention medium through co-design sessions with teachers and students. The participatory format — where children both created and performed the puppets — addressed two audiences simultaneously: the puppeteers built agency and rehearsed vulnerability in a safe container, while the audience found characters and stories that reflected their own experiences.

Scripts were co-written with students over four workshop sessions, iterating on narrative arcs that modelled healthy emotional responses without feeling prescriptive. Puppet design was intentionally abstract to allow projection and identification across a range of self-concepts.

Outcomes

Post-intervention surveys using the Rosenberg Self-Esteem Scale showed a measurable improvement in self-concept scores in both the performer and audience groups. Qualitative feedback highlighted themes of recognition, normalisation of insecurity, and a new vocabulary for discussing feelings.

The project was recognised at XXXX Conference and the methodology has since been adapted for use by the school's counselling programme.
